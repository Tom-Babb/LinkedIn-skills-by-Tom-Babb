# LinkedIn DM Style Guide

## Core Principle

These messages should read like a real person scrolling through their LinkedIn inbox, running past an old message, and firing off a quick note. Not like a marketing email. Not like a sales sequence. Not like an AI wrote it.

The person receiving this should think: "Oh cool, they actually saw my message and thought of me."

## Voice and Tone

### Do
- Write like you're texting a professional acquaintance, not drafting a press release.
- Start with their first name, nothing else. "Hey Rich," or just "Rich," — never "Dear Richard" or "Hi there!"
- Keep it to 3-5 sentences. If you're hitting 6, cut something.
- Use contractions. "I'm", "you're", "don't", "it's".
- Let sentences run a little casual. "Given you're scaling fast, thought it might be useful" is better than "Given that you are currently in a period of rapid growth, this may be of interest."
- End on the link. Don't add a sign-off, a "best," a "cheers," or a "looking forward to hearing from you." The link is the last thing. Let it breathe.

### Don't
- Don't use em dashes (—). Use commas or periods instead.
- Don't use exclamation marks more than once per message (and zero is fine).
- Don't say "I hope this message finds you well" or any variation.
- Don't say "I wanted to reach out" or "I'm reaching out."
- Don't use "excited to share" or "thrilled to announce."
- Don't write "Hi [Name], I noticed that you..." — this is the most templated-sounding opener on LinkedIn.
- Don't bullet-point the value props. This isn't a landing page.
- Don't include dates, times, or full agendas. Keep details vague enough that they have to click the link.
- Don't include speaker bios, titles, or credentials inline. If someone is important, the link will tell them.
- Don't use the phrase "I'd love to" — it's overused in LinkedIn outreach.
- Don't end with a question. End with the link.
- Don't add a sign-off (no "Best," "Cheers," "Talk soon," "Thanks,"). Just stop after the link.

## Message Structure

### The Opener (1 sentence)

Connect scrolling through DMs to relevancy. The reader should understand in the first sentence that you saw their message and thought this was relevant to them.

**Strong openers:**
- "[Name], been a while. Going through old messages and thought this might be relevant."
- "[Name], I'm looking through my dm.s and thought this might be relevant."
- "[Name], I'm going through my dm.s and ran past your message. Thought this might be relevant."
- "[Name], been a while. Going through old messages and thought this might be relevant for you."

**For people you're already in conversation with:**
- "[Name], quick heads up."
- "[Name], quick note."
- "[Name], been a minute."

**Vary these across messages.** Don't use the same opener for more than 2-3 messages in a batch. Mix "going through old messages" with "looking through my dm.s" with "ran past your message."

### The Body (2-3 sentences)

1. **What you're doing**: One sentence. Keep it high-level. "I'm doing a session on [topic]." or "We just launched [thing]." No dates, no times, no full descriptions.
2. **A few details**: One sentence with 2-3 specific things that would matter to a decision-maker. Use commas to list them inline, not bullets.
3. **Why it's relevant to them**: One sentence connecting it to their world. This is where the company name or role reference goes, toward the end of the message, not the beginning.

### The Link (bare URL)

Drop it on its own line. No "Click here" or "Register at" or "Learn more:". Just the URL.

## The Typo Strategy

This is critical. Intentional typos make messages feel human. LinkedIn DMs from real people have typos. Perfectly polished messages feel automated.

### Two Typo Types

**Type A: The "dm.s" typo (~30% of messages)**
Use "dm.s" instead of "DMs" somewhere in the message. This is a subtle, consistent quirk that reads as someone typing on their phone or just not caring about the abbreviation format. It's a stylistic tic, not a mistake.

When using this type: no other typos, no correction follow-up.

**Type B: The fix-a-word typo (~70% of messages)**
Misspell one common word in the message, then send a brief correction as a separate follow-up message. This mimics the very human behavior of sending a message, noticing a typo, and quickly correcting it.

When using this type: do NOT also use "dm.s" in the same message. Pick one or the other.

### Fix-a-Word Rules

- The typo should be a plausible keyboard slip, not gibberish. Swap two adjacent letters, drop a letter, or double a letter.
- Good typos: "oeprating" → "operating", "talnet" → "talent", "bilding" → "building", "busines" → "business", "companes" → "companies", "rethnk" → "rethink", "measrue" → "measure", "autoamte" → "automate", "advantge" → "advantage", "actualy" → "actually"
- The correction is a separate message containing only: `*[correct word]`
- Don't misspell the person's name, their company name, or any proper noun. Only common words.
- Don't misspell the same word across multiple messages. Vary which word gets the typo.

### Distribution

In a batch of 10 messages:
- ~7 should use Type B (fix-a-word with correction)
- ~3 should use Type A (dm.s, no correction)
- Never use both in the same message

## The Reference to the Recipient

### How to reference who they are

DO NOT repeat back what someone said in their message. Don't quote them, don't paraphrase their pitch, don't summarize their self-description. Instead, extract ONE insight from their data and transform it into something relevant to the ask.

**Bad:** "I saw you're building unmanned all-electric cargo aircraft capable of carrying 350 lbs at speeds up to 140 MPH."
**Good:** "Given what you're building in hardware."

**Bad:** "You mentioned you work with 800 CTOs to align their engineering teams with strategic business objectives."
**Good:** "I know you're deep in the world of CTOs and tech leaders."

**Bad:** "I saw your message about wanting to hire Gauntlet engineers."
**Good:** "Given you're leading an eng team."

### Where to place the reference

The reference to the recipient goes in the LAST sentence, not the first. Connect "scrolling through DMs" to "thought this might be relevant" at the TOP. Put the company name or role specifics at the BOTTOM, right before the link.

**Structure:**
```
[Opener connecting DMs to relevance]
[What you're doing — vague, no dates]
[A few details about the content]
[Why it's relevant → company/role reference here]. ← LAST SENTENCE
[Link]
```

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

These are the most common failure modes. Check every message against this list.

1. **The Template Feel**: If you read three messages in a row and they sound like the same message with names swapped out, rewrite them. This isn't just a quality issue. LinkedIn actively detects duplicate and near-duplicate messages. If the platform sees the same message going out to multiple people, it will throttle or restrict the sender's account. Every message must be structurally different, not just different names plugged into the same shell.
2. **The Sales Pitch**: If the message reads like it's trying to convince someone, it's too long and too detailed. Pull back. The link does the convincing.
3. **The Recap**: If you're spending more than 5 words referencing what the recipient said in their message, you're recapping. Stop.
4. **The Calendar Invite**: If the message includes a date, time, and agenda, it reads like a calendar invite, not a DM. Remove the date and time. Let the link handle logistics.
5. **The Sign-Off**: If the message ends with "Best," or "Cheers," or "Looking forward to connecting," delete it. End on the link.
6. **The Double Typo**: If a message has both "dm.s" AND a fix-a-word typo, pick one and remove the other.
7. **The Overreach**: If you're referencing a detail about the recipient that they'd have to scroll back years to remember sharing, it feels stalkerish. Keep references to things that are obviously part of their professional identity (title, company, industry), not deep-cut details from old messages.

## Formatting the Output

Each message should be presented as:

```
**[Number]. [Full Name]** — [very brief context]
[LinkedIn Profile URL]

> [Message body]
>
> [Landing page URL with UTM]

> *[corrected word, if Type B typo]
```

The brief context after the name is for the SENDER's reference (so they know who this person is at a glance). Keep it to 5-7 words: "CEO, Acme Corp" or "PE operating partner, Austin" or "ex-Dribbble, angel investor."
